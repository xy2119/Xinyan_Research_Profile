Ongoing Research

Geometric Deep Learning on Protein Design

MEng Material Science and Engineering Master Thesis

Supervisor: Prof. Stefano Angioletti-Uberti | Theory and Simulation of Materials

Protein Structure | Group Representation Theory | Rotation Invariant | Spherical Harmonics

It is worth questioning whether the current trend towards using multiple heads of pairwise attention, overparameterized MLP sub-layers, and continuously scaled up models is a sustainable or optimal approach for all tasks, particularly in the context of biomolecules. While these techniques may have achieved impressive results in some cases, they may also be unnecessarily complex or resource-intensive, and may not always generalize well to new data. To improve the efficiency and precision of biomolecule prediction models, it may be necessary to explore alternative approaches that more explicitly encode the inherent structure and properties of biomolecules, and that incorporate domain knowledge and expert insights. This could involve the use of more specialized architectures, features, and loss functions, which may be better suited to capturing the relevant features and patterns in the data, and could result in models with stronger inductive bias.

Protein design is the process of creating or modifying proteins with specific functions or properties through laboratory experiments or computational methods. It represents a grand challenge in molecular biology and biochemistry, as it requires a deep understanding of how a protein's three-dimensional structure and function are encoded in its amino acid sequence. By enabling the "custom-building" of proteins at will, protein design has the potential to create new drugs, enzymes, and other biomolecules with functions useful in biotechnology and medicine.

Recent advances in data-driven approaches have opened up new possibilities for protein design by enabling the learning of sequence-structure relationships. This represents an exciting opportunity to make progress in cases where traditional methods based on first principles may be limited. Efforts in this field are increasingly directed towards the design of new biological functions and their applications, with most contributions focusing on structural representations. One of the key challenges in protein design is the lack of robustness to the rotational degrees of freedom of the protein structure.

In this project, we present an approach for building rotation invariant representations for the inverse protein folding problem using spherical harmonics. Spherical harmonics are a set of functions defined on the surface of a sphere. We show that by specific combination of spherical harmonics, a representation of a protein structure can be built that effectively describes the symmetry, is invariant to rotations, and has lower dimensions, allowing for more accurate prediction of the amino acid sequence from the three-dimensional structure. The proposed approach is evaluated on the ProteinMPNN, and compared with a number of benchmark molecular descriptors to prove effectiveness of this method for protein design.
